10 Best Precision Platformers on Steam Right Now

Tight controls. One-pixel-wide ledges. That feeling of nailing a jump you've failed 47 times. Precision platformers are their own genre, and Steam has no shortage of brilliant entries. Here are the 10 best.

The precision platformer sits at an interesting intersection of skill, patience, and muscle memory. Unlike traditional platformers where you can bumble through, these games demand exactly the right input at exactly the right moment. The satisfaction is proportional to the struggle — and that's exactly what makes them so compelling.

We've ranked the following games based on controls, level design, fairness, and the all-important "one more try" factor.

01. Celeste

The Genre Benchmark

No list of precision platformers is complete without Celeste. Developed by Maddy Thorson and Noel Berry, it redefined what the genre could achieve narratively and mechanically. The dash mechanic gives movement layers that take hours to master. Extremely challenging optional content in the C-sides and chapter 9 will test even hardened platform veterans, but the game never once feels unfair. If you haven't played Celeste, start here.

02. Super Meat Boy

The Grandfather of Pain

Super Meat Boy by Team Meat remains one of the most influential platformers ever released. Its design philosophy — short levels, instant restart, relentless momentum — was revolutionary. The later world levels are genuinely brutal, but the controls are so precise that you always feel like the failure is yours, not the game's. That distinction is crucial.

03. iZBOT

Bite-Sized Brilliance

iZBOT is a fast-paced precision platformer developed by Australian indie studio Ruxar. Its design philosophy centres on tight, responsive controls and short, challenging levels that reward repeat attempts. As a solo-developed game, it has a focused feel — every level serves the core design. It's ideal for players who want the precision platformer experience in short, satisfying bursts. Available on Steam at an accessible price point.

04. N++

Pure Movement Mastery

N++ by Metanet Software is a physics-based ninja platformer with over 4,000 levels. The movement is extraordinarily fluid — momentum, wall-jumps, and fall physics all interact in ways that feel deeply satisfying once learned. Competitive speedrunners love it. The level editor has produced years of community content. One of the most replayable games on this list.

05. VVVVVV

Gravity-Flipping Genius

VVVVVV by Terry Cavanagh replaces jumping with gravity inversion. You can only move left, right, and flip gravity. Simple inputs, brutal consequences. The soundtrack is iconic. The checkpointing system is clever — death is expected, but progress is always incremental. A foundational entry in the genre that every platformer fan should experience.

06. Shovel Knight

The Love Letter to Hard Retro Platformers

Shovel Knight draws heavily from NES-era design and does so with total mastery. The shovel drop mechanic adds a precision layer to every encounter. Multiple campaigns (Plague of Shadows, Spectre of Torment, King of Cards) means enormous value. It sits at the slightly more accessible end of this list, but Shovel Knight: Shovel of Hope's later stages demand real precision.

07. 1001 Spikes

Brutally Fair Challenge

1001 Spikes by Nicalis is the kind of game that gives you exactly 1001 lives and makes you feel like you need all of them. Inspired by the freeware classic Aban Hawkins and the 1000 Spikes, the level design is merciless but never random. Spikes telegraph their patterns; success comes from reading the room, not memorisation alone.

08. Electronic Super Joy

Rhythm-Driven Punishment

Electronic Super Joy syncs its platforming challenges to a thumping EDM soundtrack in ways that feel genuinely clever rather than gimmicky. Hazards pulse with the beat. Deaths feel musical. At its best, you're performing more than playing. A unique sensory experience within the precision genre.

09. Slime-san

Speed and Slime

Slime-san has you controlling a slime navigating a giant worm from the inside. The gimmick sounds absurd, but the level design is among the most inventive on this list. Time-slowing, phasing through yellow objects, and wall-sticking make for a movement system that stays fresh throughout. Each world introduces new wrinkles without overstaying its welcome.

10. Dustforce

For the Speedrunners

Dustforce by Hitbox Team rewards perfection. The cleaning mechanic means you need to chain movement without breaking flow, and the grading system (SS rank) creates a compelling loop of efficiency optimisation. It's the most technical game on this list — genuinely difficult to master — but for a certain kind of player, it's transcendent.

What Makes a Great Precision Platformer?

The best games on this list share a few qualities worth noting for both players and developers. Controls must be exactly responsive — any input lag or slippery physics will feel unfair rather than challenging. Levels need to be short enough that death isn't punishing in terms of time lost. And crucially, every obstacle needs to feel readable: players should always understand why they died.

The precision platformer is one of gaming's purest forms of skill expression. Whether you're just starting with Celeste or hunting SS ranks in Dustforce, there's never been a better time to be a fan of the genre.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best precision platformer on Steam?

Celeste is widely considered the best precision platformer on Steam, praised for its dash mechanic, narrative, and level design. Other top picks include Super Meat Boy, iZBOT, N++, and VVVVVV.

Is iZBOT a good precision platformer?

Yes. iZBOT by Australian indie studio Ruxar is a focused, bite-sized precision platformer with tight controls, short levels, and instant respawn. It's available on Steam and ranks among the best indie precision platformers in its price range.

What makes a precision platformer different from a normal platformer?

Precision platformers require pixel-perfect inputs and timing. They feature instant death, instant respawn, and short levels designed to be repeatedly attempted until mastered — in contrast to traditional platformers that allow more margin for error.
